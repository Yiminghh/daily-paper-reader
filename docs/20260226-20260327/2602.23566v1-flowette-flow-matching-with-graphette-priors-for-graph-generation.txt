# Flowette: Flow Matching with Graphette Priors for Graph Generation 

Asiri Wijesinghe 1 Sevvandi Kandanaarachchi 1 Daniel M. Steinberg 1 Cheng Soon Ong 1 2 

Abstract 

We study generative modeling of graphs with re-curring subgraph motifs. We propose Flowette, a continuous flow matching framework, that em-ploys a graph neural network based transformer to learn a velocity field defined over graph rep-resentations with node and edge attributes. Our model preserves topology through optimal trans-port based coupling, and long-range structural dependencies through regularisation. To incorpo-rate domain driven structural priors, we introduce graphettes, a new probabilistic family of graph structure models that generalize graphons via con-trolled structural edits for motifs like rings, stars and trees. We theoretically analyze the coupling, invariance, and structural properties of the pro-posed framework, and empirically evaluate it on synthetic and small-molecule graph generation tasks. Flowette demonstrates consistent improve-ments, highlighting the effectiveness of combin-ing structural priors with flow-based training for modeling complex graph distributions. 

1. Introduction 

Generative modeling of graphs has applications spanning molecular design, social networks, biological systems, and combinatorial optimization (You et al., 2018; Kipf & Welling, 2016; Zhu et al., 2022). Graphs exhibit complex structure, permutation symmetry, and rich higher-order mo-tifs such as cycles, hubs, and repeated subgraphs. These motifs encode essential domain semantics. For instance, molecular graphs are characterized by ring structures and valence constraints. Existing approaches rely on domain-specific architectures, resulting in specialized models for each motif (Jin et al., 2018; Shi et al., 2020; Karami, 2023). This limits transferability and obscures the underlying prin-ciples of structure-aware graph generation. Recent progress in continuous-time generative modeling-

Flow matching (FM) directly learns a velocity field transporting samples from a source distribution to the data distribution (Lipman et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). However, existing graph FM approaches typi-cally rely on implicit or independent coupling between noise and data graphs, ignoring structural compatibility under per-mutation and size variability. As a result of forcing models to interpolate between topologically mismatched graphs, velocity supervision can be high-variance and inconsistent. Three ingredients are essential for structure-aware flow-based graph generation. First, supervision pairs in flow matching should be structurally aligned. Second, the train-ing objective must explicitly regularize global trajectory coherence and domain feasibility. Third, the source dis-tribution itself should encode meaningful structural priors, reflecting the dominant motifs. We introduce Flowette, a graph generation framework that contains these three ingredients. (1) Flowette couples noise graphs and data graphs using fused Gromov-Wasserstein (FGW) optimal transport (Titouan et al., 2019), yield-ing node permutation-consistent correspondences across variable-sized graphs. By providing supervision pairs that respect topology, we enable structure aligned flow match-ing on graphs. The velocity field is parameterized by a topology respecting Graph Neural Network (GNN) based transformer that jointly evolves node features, edge features, and adjacency values. (2) We introduce regularisation terms in the training objective to preserve long range structures and permutation equivariance. The resulting training objec-tive leads to more stable continuous time generation and improved structural fidelity. (3) To enable structural induc-tive biases in the source distribution, we introduce a new mathematical object graphettes , a probabilistic family of graph priors that generalize graphons via controlled edits. Graphettes allow principled injection or removal of mo-tifs (rings, stars, cycles), providing a unified mechanism to model dense, sparse, and motif-rich graphs within a single framework. Unlike task-specific heuristics, graphettes de-couple structural assumptions from the neural architecture. 1

2. Background and setting 

Graphs have interchangeable representations, and we use the following conventions. A graph G with n vertices (nodes) can be written in terms of vertices and edges, (V, E ), or equivalently by its A ∈ { 0, 1}n×n binary adjacency matrix. We retrieve the sets of vertices and edges in G by V (G)

and E(G) respectively and denote the number of vertices by |V (G)|.When we need to model attributed graphs with node and edge features, we use X ∈ Rn×dx for node features and 

F ∈ Rn×n×df for edge features of G. Sec. 3 introduces the flow matching framework for attributed graphs, and we denote a graph G by (A, X, F ), a triple consisting of the adjacency matrix, node and edge features of G. In Sec. 4 we introduce graphette priors focusing on non-attributed graphs G, which we denote by (V, E ). We use G ∼ W to indicate that a graph is sampled from some distribution or using a sampling function W.

2.1. Flow Matching on Graphs Related Work. Graph generation has need to capture local and global structures, as motivated by domains like molecu-lar design, combinatorial optimisation, and biological net-works (Mercado et al., 2021; Sun & Yang, 2023; Yi et al., 2023). Existing methods can be broadly grouped into autore-gressive (You et al., 2018; Liao et al., 2019; Shi et al., 2020; Goyal et al., 2020) and one-shot paradigms (Dai et al., 2020; Shirzad et al., 2022; Karami, 2023; Davies et al., 2023). Diffusion-based generative models have recently become the dominant one-shot paradigm for graphs (Niu et al., 2020; Jo et al., 2022b), but discrete time versions tie sampling tra-jectory to the training schedule. Continuous-time discrete diffusion models based on Continuous Time Markov Chains (CTMCs) address this rigidity by decoupling the sampling grid from the training objective (Campbell et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2024a). DisCo (Xu et al., 2024a), for instance, defines a discrete-state continuous-time diffusion on graphs and inherits the permutation-equivariant/invariant properties of DiGress (Vignac et al., 2022) while enabling flexible nu-merical integration and theoretical control of approximation error (Xu et al., 2024a). Discrete flow matching (DFM) extends FM to discrete state spaces, where probability paths are defined by time-inhomogeneous CTMCs and the model parameterises transition rates rather than continuous veloc-ities (Campbell et al., 2024; Gat et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2025). In parallel, some works have explored continuous FM and flow-based formulations for graphs by relaxing discrete structures into continuous spaces (Liu et al., 2019; Eijkelboom et al., 2024b). However, they typically rely on implicit couplings between source and target graphs. More discussion of related work is in Appendix A. 

Flow matching offers an alternative to diffusion-based training for continuous-time generative models (Lipman et al., 2023). Rectified-flow variants adopt linear inter-polants, and regress the associated constant conditional velocity field (Liu et al., 2023), leading to state-of-the-art results in high-dimensional image and sequence generation (Esser et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024). 3. Structure Preserving Flows 

We visualise our overall design in Figure 1. In this sec-tion we address key challenges that arise when learning continuous-time generative dynamics on graphs and clarify how our method departs from the standard flow-matching pipeline. First, naively coupling noise and data graphs (e.g., by minibatch index) ignores structural compatibil-ity and leads to high-variance supervision since the velocity field must interpolate between topologically mismatched source-target pairs. Second, unconstrained velocity fields defined over continuous graph representations can yield in-valid structures under finite-step integration, particularly for molecular graphs where chemical constraints must be respected. To address these issues, we have three core com-ponents: (i) a structure-aware coupling mechanism for su-pervision (Sec. 3.1), which replaces independent or (Eu-clidean) optimal transport coupling with FGW-distance and Hungarian graph matching; (ii) a permutation-equivariant GNN transformer that parameterizes the velocity field over evolving graph representations (Sec. 3.2 and App. B); and (iii) an enhanced training objective (Sec. 3.3) that aug-ments the flow-matching loss with endpoint consistency and chemistry-aware structural regularization. Together, these design choices yield coherent, permutation-invariant super-vision across variable-sized graphs and promote structurally valid generations under finite-step integration. In this sec-tion we use G to refer to attributed graphs, G = ( A, X, F )

and we present the full training scheme in Algorithm 1.

4.1. The Graphette

4.2. Rich and recurring subgraph motifs 

We can choose a graph edit function f to suit our purpose. If a graphon W can generate the graphs we require, such as stochastic block model graphs, we can let f be the identity function, making no modifications to the graph. If we are modeling social networks, then f can add stars. Ring ad-dition is useful to model molecular networks as they have ring structures such as benzene. We use the following graph edit functions (GEF) depending on problem domain. 

GEF 1: Identity . Let f = I be the identity function with 

I(G) = G, which does not edit the graph. 

This is used in App. F to show that graphettes can recover graphons and sparsified graphons. Furthermore, for stochas-tic block models, as illustrated in Section 5.1 graphettes are equivalent to graphons and our Flowette model achieves state of the art performance. 

GEF 2: Cycle deletion . Let f = h denote a global function that removes cycles from the graph. 

When the application requires that graphs are trees (no cy-cles), we choose f = h. For a connected graph the resulting graph is a tree. The class of graphettes also includes graph edit functions which may be parametrised, for example we may wish to model different ring sizes in molecular graphs in Sec. 5.2. 

GEF 3: Ring addition . Let R(p, c ) denote a function where a ring of size c is added and connected to a node i with probability p, i.e., R(p, c ) = Bernoulli( p) × c.

For social network graphs, we model hubs, which are highly connected individuals. 

GEF 4: Star addition . Let S (a, b, {ui}ni=1 ) denote a function that adds stars given by a Poisson random vari-able Poi( an exp( ui + b)) to a node i where {ui}ni=1 =(u1, . . . , u n), 0 ≤ ui ≤ 1, a > 0 and |b| < 1.

This models a non-uniform probability of any particular node i (indexed by ui) , having a star added, with star sizes varying with ui.

Remark 4.2 . Some characteristics of graphettes are explored in Appendix F. In particular, graphettes can recover graphon 

W and the sparsified graphon Wn, have the ability to gen-erate graphs similar to those generated by graphexes and generate dense graphs from a family of graphons. Further-more, the ring function GEF 3 can generate dense or sparse graphs depending on {ρi}i (see Lemmas in Appendix F).

5.3. Ablation Analysis of Regularization Terms 

To evaluate the role of each component in the Flowette train-ing objective, we conduct an ablation study by selectively removing its key elements. Table 6 reports an ablation study of the individual components of the Flowette training ob-jective in Eq. (8) on QM9 and ZINC250K. Overall, the results demonstrate that each term plays a distinct and com-plementary role. Endpoint consistency ( Lend ) and valence regularization ( Lval ) are the most critical components for stable and chemically valid generation, while atom-type marginal matching ( Latom ) primarily improves diversity. The chemistry-aware terms Lval and Latom primarily gov-ern feasibility and diversity. Following endpoint consis-tency, removing valence supervision ( no Lval ) causes a pronounced validity drop on both datasets, demonstrating the importance of soft valence constraints for preventing over-bonding. In contrast, removing atom-type marginal matching ( no Latom ) has a milder effect on validity, unique-ness and novelty. Removing both terms ( only Lvel and 

Lend ) yields intermediate performance, confirming that the two regularizers provide complementary signals. Veloc-ity matching alone is insufficient, and strong performance emerges only when all components are jointly enforced. More discussion of the other ablations are in Appendix H.7. 

Observed limitations While Flowette improves stability and structural fidelity in flow-based graph generation, sev-eral limitations remain. First, FGW-coupling introduces additional computational overhead, particularly as graph size or batch size increases. Second, although graphette priors provide interpretable and flexible structural bias, their design requires domain knowledge, limiting full automa-tion across arbitrary graph families. Finally, continuous relaxation of adjacency and edge attributes requires a pro-jection step at generation time, which may introduce minor discretization artifacts. 

6. Conclusion 

We propose a generative model, Flowette, for graphs with recurring sub-graph motifs. Our generative model contains three essential components for structure-aware flow-based graph generation: (1) structural alignment of supervision pairs using fused Gromov-Wasserstein optimal transport; (2) training objective that explicitly regularises towards global trajectory coherence and chemical feasibility (for molecule generation); (3) source distributions that encode structural priors. Flowette imposes structural inductive bias by graphette structural priors, where we have intro-duced a novel mathematical object, graphettes, which extend graphons by editing them with sub-graph motifs. Graphettes support injection of rings and stars, as well as cycle removal to model trees. The velocity field is learned by a GNN based transformer, and Flowette demonstrates state of the art per-formance on empirical benchmarks. Theoretical analysis of graphettes and ablation studies on Flowette provide deeper insight into alignment, invariance, and structural properties. We hope that each of the three proposed key components, structural alignment, global coherence regularization, and graphette priors, will independently be useful to further developments in graph generation. 8Flowette: Flow Matching with Graphette Priors

A. Historical Background and Related Work on Flow Matching 

A.1. Graph generative models 

Graph generative modeling aims to learn distributions over graphs that capture both local connectivity patterns and global structural properties, with applications in molecular design, combinatorial optimisation, and biological networks (Mercado et al., 2021; Sun & Yang, 2023; Yi et al., 2023). Existing methods can be broadly grouped into autoregressive and one-shot paradigms. Autoregressive models sequentially grow a graph by inserting nodes and edges according to a chosen ordering (You et al., 2018; Liao et al., 2019; Shi et al., 2020; Goyal et al., 2020). This sequential view allows fine-grained control and the injection of domain-specific constraints (e.g. valency checks in molecular generation), but comes at a cost: models must either learn an effective node/edge ordering or commit to a hand-crafted one, which enlarges the hypothesis space and breaks permutation invariance (Kong et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023). As a result, autoregressive approaches tend to be computationally expensive, difficult to parallelise, and brittle on graphs with complex long-range dependencies. To mitigate these issues, a large body of work has explored one-shot generation, where all graph variables are predicted in a single pass. One-shot models instantiate graph-adapted versions of VAEs, GANs, and normalising flows (Kipf & Welling, 2016; De Cao & Kipf, 2018; Liu et al., 2019). While they restore permutation equivariance/ invariance and avoid learning an ordering, purely one-shot models often underfit rich higher-order structure unless equipped with carefully engineered architectures or hierarchical decompositions (Dai et al., 2020; Shirzad et al., 2022; Karami, 2023; Davies et al., 2023). 

Graph diffusion models. Diffusion-based generative models have recently become the dominant one-shot paradigm for graphs. Early work adapted continuous diffusion frameworks to graph-structured data by treating adjacency and features as continuous variables (Niu et al., 2020; Jo et al., 2022b), which simplifies optimisation but departs from the discrete nature of graph topology. Subsequent methods introduced discrete-state diffusion over node and edge categories using discrete-time Markov chains (Austin et al., 2021; Vignac et al., 2022; Haefeli et al., 2022), achieving strong performance on benchmark graph and molecular datasets. However, these models tie the sampling trajectory to the training schedule: the number of reverse steps must match the (fixed) number of forward noising steps, and sampling cost is therefore tightly coupled to training hyperparameters. Continuous-time discrete diffusion models based on Continuous Time Markov Chains (CTMCs) address this rigidity by decoupling the sampling grid from the training objective (Campbell et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2024a). DisCo (Xu et al., 2024a), for instance, defines a discrete-state continuous-time diffusion on graphs and inherits the permutation-equivariant/invariant properties of DiGress while enabling flexible numerical integration and theoretical control of approximation error (Xu et al., 2024a). Other variants exploit degree information or hierarchical decompositions to improve scalability and sample quality (Chen et al., 2023; Bergmeister et al., 2023; Limnios et al., 2023). Despite these advances, diffusion-based graph models still rely on carefully designed rate matrices or noise schedules, and their local node/edge updates make it difficult to preserve global topology and higher-order motifs without additional inductive biases or auxiliary constraints. 

Graph flow matching models. Flow matching (FM) offers an efficient alternative to diffusion-based training for continuous-time generative models. Rather than learning to reverse a fixed stochastic process, FM directly regresses a time-dependent vector field whose trajectories transport a simple source distribution to a complex target distribution along a chosen probability path (Lipman et al., 2023). Rectified-flow variants further simplify training by adopting linear interpolants between data and noise and regressing the associated constant conditional velocity field (Liu et al., 2023), leading to state-of-the-art results in high-dimensional image and sequence generation (Esser et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024). Extending FM to discrete state spaces has given rise to discrete flow matching (DFM), where probability paths are defined by time-inhomogeneous CTMCs and the model parameterises transition rates rather than continuous velocities (Campbell et al., 2024; Gat et al., 2024). Compared to discrete diffusion, DFM replaces hand-designed forward rate matrices with learnable dynamics and decouples the choice of interpolation path from the training objective. Building on this, DeFoG introduces the first DFM-based framework for graph generation, combining CTMC-based flows with a graph transformer backbone and a training–sampling decoupling that improves flexibility over purely diffusion-based methods (Qin et al., 2025). In parallel, some works have explored continuous FM and flow-based formulations for graphs by relaxing discrete structures into continuous spaces. Graph normalising flows and related models learn invertible transports over continuous embeddings of adjacency and attributes (Liu et al., 2019), while variational flow matching for graph generation embeds graphs into continuous latent spaces and trains vector fields via a variational objective (Eijkelboom et al., 2024b). These approaches demonstrate that FM-style training can be competitive to diffusion-based graph generators in terms of efficiency and 12 Flowette: Flow Matching with Graphette Priors 

flexibility. However, they typically rely on implicit couplings between source and target graphs (e.g. through shared encoders or attention over batched graphs) and do not explicitly enforce structural alignment under permutation and size variability. Consequently, velocity supervision can become inconsistent across permutations or mismatched graph components, making it difficult to guarantee stable preservation of global topology and higher-order motifs during integration. Our work situates itself in this emerging line of flow-matching graph generators, but departs from prior art in two key ways: (1) we adopt a continuous FM framework over relaxed adjacency and node features while explicitly aligning noise and data graphs via structure-aware matching, and (2) we incorporate principled structural priors into both the source distribution and the training objective to capture local and global structural properties. This design yields coherent, permutation-invariant velocity supervision across variable-sized graphs and enables Flowette to learn stable continuous-time graph transformations that preserve rich combinatorial structure beyond what is typically captured by existing diffusion- or flow-based models. 

Beyond graphons for sparse graphs. As a consequence of the Aldous-Hoover theorem (Aldous, 1981) graphs sampled from graphons are dense, i.e., the number of edges grow quadratically with the number of nodes. However, many realworld graphs such as social and molecular networks are sparse, i.e., the number of edges grow subquadratically with the nodes. Graphexes (Veitch & Roy, 2015) and graph sparsification (Klopp et al., 2017) are two popular methods that are used for sparse graph modelling. Graphexes, introduced by Veitch & Roy (2015) have the functionality to add stars and isolated edges to a graph sampled by a graphon and have rich mathematical underpinnings (Veitch & Roy, 2019; Caron et al., 2023). Sparsified graphons are based on the simple fact that sparse graph sequences converge to the zero graphon W = 0 . However, a graph sampled from W = 0 is not representative of sparse graphs as it contains only nodes without any edges. To rectify this issue, sparsified graphons propose a sequence {ρn}n converging to zero and the graphs are sampled from ρnW , where 

W̸ = 0 . Sparsified graphons are used in many settings including Keriven et al. (2020); Klopp et al. (2017).

D. Computational Complexity Analysis 

Let B be the batch size, n the graph size used for flow matching, m the number of edges |E| in the graph, L the number of transformer layers, H the hidden dimension, and K the number of Euler steps. 

FGW alignment. Computing the FGW cost matrix between B noise and B target graphs requires B2 FGW solves. Using a square-loss FGW solver with I iterations, this costs O(B2In 3), plus O(B2m(dx + dz )) to form feature and structure costs; Hungarian matching adds O(B3). These computations can be embarrassingly parallel and precomputed offline once per dataset. 

Flow-matching training. After FGW precomputation, each minibatch contains B matched graph pairs. Edge-aware flow matching costs O(LmH 2 + md f ) per pair, yielding a per-minibatch cost O(B(LmH 2 + md f )) . Training over R epochs and M minibatches therefore costs O(RM B (LmH 2 + md f )) .

H. Details of Empirical Evaluation 

H.1. Discretisation and chemical validity. 

At sampling time only, the final continuous output is deterministically mapped to a discrete molecular graph: atom and bond types are decoded via argmax over categorical logits, the adjacency matrix is interpreted as an edge-confidence score, and chemical feasibility is ensured through valence-aware pruning that removes low-confidence bonds when valence constraints are violated. Importantly, this projection is applied exclusively at inference and does not affect training or gradient flow. In contrast to diffusion-based molecular generators such as DiGress (Vignac et al., 2022) and DisCo (Xu et al., 2024a), which typically rely on post-hoc validity checks or rejection of invalid samples after denoising, our approach guarantees chemical validity through a deterministic terminal projection without altering the learned dynamics or discarding samples. 

H.2. Evaluation Metrics for Synthetic Graph Evaluation 

Generation quality is assessed using different metrics: (1) degree distribution (Deg.), (2) clustering coefficient (Clus.), and (3) orbit count (Orbit) statistics. For all metrics, we quantify the discrepancy between the generated and test distributions using Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) (Gretton et al., 2012; Martinkus et al., 2022; Bergmeister et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2025). These discrepancies are summarized using the Ratio metric (lower is better). We also report generation quality using validity (V), uniqueness (U), novelty (N), and their combined V.U.N. metric. Following prior work (Qin et al., 2025), we generate the same number of graphs as in the test set (40 samples) to enable a fair comparison of graph statistic distributions. We evaluate generative quality by comparing the distributions of key graph statistics computed on generated samples against 28 Flowette: Flow Matching with Graphette Priors 

those of the test set. Specifically, we consider degree distributions, clustering coefficients, and orbit counts. Distributional discrepancies are quantified using the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). Specifically, we first compute MMD values between the training and test sets for the same statistics, establishing a reference level of distributional shift. We then compute MMD values between the generated and test sets. The Ratio is defined as the average generated:test MMD normalized by the average training:test MMD, providing a scale-free measure of how closely the generated graphs match the test distribution. H.3. Hyperparameter settings for Synthetic Graph Evaluation 

For synthetic graph benchmarks, we use a simplified training objective by setting βval = 0 and βatom = 0 , thereby excluding chemistry-specific regularization terms that are not applicable to purely structural graph data. We optimize all models using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019). For synthetic graph benchmarks, models are trained for 100 epochs with a learning rate of 1e−3, hidden dimension H = 128 , and batch size B = 32 . We set the βend = 1 .0 and λx = λe = 0 .5,and use an FGW trade-off parameter α = 0 .5. Euler integration is performed with K = 50 steps at sampling time. For the network architecture, we use L = 3 attention layers for the Tree and Ego-small datasets, and L = 4 attention layers for the SBM dataset.

H.7. Ablation Analysis of Regularization Terms 

To evaluate the role of each component in the Flowette training objective, we conduct an ablation study by selectively removing its key elements. • NoReg/ only Lvel : This variant removes all regularization terms and only keeps Lvel .• NoValenceReg/ no Lval : This variant removes only the regularization term Lval .• NoAtomReg/ no Latom : This variant removes only the regularization term Latom .• NoEndReg/ no Lend : This variant removes only the regularization term Lend .• NoChemReg/ only Lvel and Lend : This variant removes both chemistry-aware regularization terms Lval and Latom .Table 6 reports an ablation study of the individual components of the Flowette training objective in Eq. (8) on QM9 and ZINC250K. Overall, the results demonstrate that each term plays a distinct and complementary role, and that strong generative performance emerges only when all components are jointly enforced. 

Effect of removing regularization (NoReg). Training with Lvel alone leads to a substantial drop in validity on both datasets, indicating that unconstrained continuous transport is insufficient for molecular graph generation. While the learned flow interpolates between noise and data, finite-step integration frequently produces chemically invalid structures, highlighting the need for additional structural supervision. 

Endpoint consistency (NoEndReg). Removing Lend significantly degrades validity and uniqueness, confirming that locally accurate velocity predictions do not reliably compose into globally coherent transport trajectories. Endpoint supervision is therefore critical for stabilizing continuous-time generation under finite-step solvers. 

Chemistry-aware regularization. The chemistry-aware terms primarily govern feasibility and diversity. Removing valence supervision ( NoValenceReg ) causes a pronounced validity drop, especially on ZINC250K, demonstrating the importance of soft valence constraints for preventing over-bonding. In contrast, removing atom-type marginal matching ( NoAtomReg )has a milder effect on validity but consistently reduces uniqueness and novelty, indicating its role in promoting semantic diversity rather than enforcing hard constraints. Removing both terms ( NoChemReg ) yields intermediate performance, confirming that the two regularizers provide complementary signals. Overall, endpoint consistency and valence regularization are the most critical components for stable and chemically valid generation, while atom-type marginal matching primarily improves diversity.
