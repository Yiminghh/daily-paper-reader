## Abstract 

Discrete graph generation has emerged as a powerful paradigm for modeling graph data, often relying on highly expressive neu-ral backbones such as transformers or higher-order architectures. We revisit this design choice by introducing GenGNN, a modular message-passing framework for graph generation. Diffusion models with GenGNN achieve more than 90% validity on Tree and Planar datasets, within margins of graph transformers, at 2-5x faster in-ference speed. For molecule generation, DiGress with a GenGNN backbone achieves 99.49% Validity. A systematic ablation study shows the benefit provided by each GenGNN component, indicat-ing the need for residual connections to mitigate oversmoothing on complicated graph-structure. Through scaling analyses, we apply a principled metric-space view to investigate learned diffusion rep-resentations and uncover whether GNNs can be expressive neural backbones for discrete diffusion.

Recent work integrates continuous-time modeling into discrete diffusion via flow matching [ 45 , 58 ], where further decoupling of training and sampling results in near-perfect performance on some benchmark datasets [39]. In order for discrete diffusion models to achieve best-in-class results, the current landscape for graph generative models (GGMs) rely on specialized graph encoder backbones. This includes variants of either the transformer architecture [ 35 , 40 , 50 ] or maximally-expressive graph networks [ 32 , 34 ]. The need for integrating ex-pressive neural backbones into GGMs relies on observations from prior work and is distilled into a simple two-part intuition. One ,message-passing within less-expressive graph networks fail to dis-tinguish simple isomorphic substructures [ 55 ]. Two , graph neural networks (GNNs) suffer from over-smoothing, which translates to failure when scaling on long-range tasks [ 2]. This lack of expressiv-ity implies that simple GNNs fail to accurately denoise complicated graph-structure across the many diffusion timesteps required for successful sampling, as demonstrated in Figure 1a with a simple GNN backbone on the Planar dataset versus a graph transformer backbone in Figure 1b. However, a conflicting story is apparent within the node- and graph-classification tasks, where recent work shows simpler GNNs (with modifications) achieving comparable performance to graph transformers (GTs) [ 25 , 27 , 28 ]. This is noteworthy, as simple GNNs are more efficient than expressive models reliant on tupled-based representations [ 32 , 34 , 35 , 40 ], therefore translating to inference speed improvements [ 28 ]. Any kind of speed improvement is im-portant for discrete graph diffusion, which currently struggles with scalability from the quadratic time-complexity required for graph reconstruction [ 39 , 51 ]. This leads us to the ask the question of, are expressive encoders really necessary for discrete graph diffusion?

Despite any efficiency boost promised by a simpler baseline, repeated empirical observations indicate their failure to re-create their training data within generated graphs. Therefore, a balance must be achieved between expressivity and time efficiency. To answer this, we first design GenGNN, a neural framework created to address the shortcomings of simpler GNNs as neural backbones for discrete graph diffusion. Unlike graph transformers, GenGNN relies solely on message-passing to denoise latent repre-sentations, which is further integrated into a modular framework for effective sampling of generated graphs. The message-passing restriction of GenGNN is intentional, enabling analysis to focus on the threshold at which expressiveness matters for discrete graph diffusion .Initial benchmarking indicates GenGNN achieves performance competitive with SOTA models, 99.35% validity and over 2.5x infer-ence speed-up relative to theoretically-expressive architectures on the QM9 dataset [ 54 ]. This inference speedup is comprehensively illustrated with 1.7-5x improvements and competitive performance across common benchmarks including: Tree, Planar, SBM, ZINC, Guacamol, and MOSES [ 4, 7, 33 , 38 , 47 ]. Furthermore, ablation stud-ies on the proposed GenGNN framework demonstrate the necessity of each proposed component within GenGNN. Scaling analysis across increasing number of layers for these ablated components indicates GenGNN learn latent representations [ 26 ] comparable to more-expressive architectures. We further derive theoretical rela-tionships to how these latent representations correlate to improved downstream generation within multivariate-systems, formalizing the intuition of how decoupled frameworks allow bypassing of standard expressivity hierarchies. The contribution of our work is as follows: 

• We introduce GenGNN, a modular neural message-passing framework enabling less-expressive model backbones to achieve competitive graph generation performance with 1.7-5x inference speedup. 

• We detail a theoretical underpinning as to why GenGNN enhances standard message-passing for discrete graph diffu-sion. 

• We conduct detailed analysis ablating components of the GenGNN framework and further scaling framework layers to demonstrate the connection between downstream perfor-mance and latent representations 1.

Oversmoothing in GNNs. A common concern with vanilla GNNs is that they over-smooth node signals when propagating distant information [ 1 ]. ## 3 GenGNN 

Current encoder backbones for GGMs [ 39 , 51 ] tend to use powerful but expensive model designs which hamper efficiency. On the other hand, recent work has shown that simpler models can best more complex ones for more traditional graph tasks such as node classi-fication [ 27 ] and graph tasks [ 27 , 28 ]. We are therefore interested in investigating whether similar performance can be achieved via simpler GNN models ?We therefore propose a simple GNN design that incorporates best practices [ 27 , 28 ] while still maintaining strong efficiency. Our framework, Generative Graph Neural Network framework (GenGNN), is modular neural framework designed to integrate message-passing neural networks or graph neural networks into diffusion models as effective generative backbones. This effective-ness is achieve through a stackable list of components which are integrated after initial message-passing layers.

## 4 Experiments 

We empirically demonstrate the validity of our anchored-denoiser claim through strong performance of GenGNN as a new backbone for DiGress and DeFoG across standard graph generation bench-mark datasets. We conduct our experimentation in multiple phases: 

## 4.1 Results 

Experimental Settings. We test the GenGNN framework’s ability to generate diverse graph structural and molecular characteristics with modular variants of GNN [ 12 ], GCN [ 21 ], GINe [ 55 ] versus the LocalPPGN [ 4] and GT [ 51 ] backbones across the Comm20, Planar, SBM [ 33 ], Tree [ 4 ], QM9 [ 54 ], and ZINC250K [ 47 ] datasets. Initial tuning followed the standard set by GT, with the remaining model hyperparameters fixed to the same settings. Models on synthetic datasets were selected based on best-validation MMD, whereas molecular datasets were selected based on best Validity. We apply the same datasets and testing standards as [ 39 ]. The details for each can be found in Table 6. More details on experimental settings are included in Appendix A. 

Structural and Molecular Generation. As shown in Table 1, GenGNN variants consistently improve or perform on-par with PPGN and GT across average MMD ratio, with relative gains for Tree, Pla-nar, and SBM reaching 19%, 16%, and 28% respectively. ## 4.3 Oversmoothing Analysis 

We follow the methodology proposed in [ 59 ] to further validate the extent to which residuals prevent oversmoothing. The validity ratio is calculated between validity scores at layer depth 24 over layer depth 1. Note, raw ERank [ 41 ] and NumRank [ 42 ] calculations scale with layer depth and size. The Pearson correlation between Validity and ERank/NumRank enables a standardized measure of downstream performance to internal model oversmoothing. As shown in Table 4, when residual connections are ablated, posi-tive Pearson correlation (~ 0.99 ) for ERank [ 41 ] and NumRank [ 42 ]indicates clear oversmoothing. This further translates to a lower validity ratio, where downstream graph generation degrades with more layers. The correlation to rank and validity within Table 4 also indicates when GenGNN is resilient to oversmoothing , which occurs where negative Pearson correlation and higher validity ratio (> 0.97 ) indicate limited degradation across layer depth. Further-more, validity ratios > 1.0 demonstrate a measurable performance improvement across increasing layers; establishing the need for residual connections to mitigate oversmoothing within GenGNN.

## 4.5 Metric-Space Scaling Investigation 

In order to isolate whether GenGNN learns latent representations which effectively capture the necessary modes for proper graph generation, we integrate the GINe-variant of GenGNN into the randomly-initialized feature extractor framework introduced by [ 49 ]. The feature extractor is given reference and predicted graphs as input in order to produce embeddings useful for evaluating latent diversity of learned model representations. To do this, we select the MagDiff metric, as introduced in Equation 4. MagDiff provides the practical benefit of near-perfect correlation to identifying struc-tural diversity as well high-sensitivity to mode-collapse, even with highly-noisy input samples [26]. In Figure 5, both rows of left and center subplots gradually in-crease the number of layers for: GT, a fully-enabled GenGNN base-line, and GenGNN with RRWP and Residuals ablated. The top-right subplot indicates the tradeoff between MagDiff and Validity for each diffusion backbone. The bottom-right subplot is restricted to Average MMD Ratio and Accuracy due to the Tree dataset contain-ing non-unique feature embeddings due to it’s fixed node size of 64. The scaling analysis is conducted in this way to utilize MagDiff’s sensitivity to measuring latent diversity of generated samples and correlate it to Validity and Accuracy. Both left-most subplots indi-cate GenGNN is resilient to oversmoothing. This is demonstrated in two ways: 1) GenGNN’s gradual performance increase with the number of layers, 2) the stability of GenGNN’s performance at a higher number of layers relative to GT and when residual-connections are ablated. The bottom center and rightmost subplots demonstrates that GenGNN consistently performs on-par with GT on the Tree dataset, regardless of layer depth. The top center and rightmost subplots demonstrate GenGNN often clusters around MagDiff values lower than GT, indicating the message-passing can better capture latent diversity in graph substructure for the QM9 dataset. ## 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we take aim at graph transformer backbones typically used in discrete graph generation. In practice, existing models tend to use powerful encoders, which come with the downside of being far less efficient than vanilla GNNs. We propose GenGNN, a simple and modular GNN framework which incorporates best practices to demonstrate comparable performance to expressive systems with improved efficiency. Across a variety of real-world benchmarks GenGNN achieves similar if not better performance, at more than 2-5x the inference speed. Further analysis shows GenGNN is capable of capturing substructures. We believe this opens exciting future work with faster and more performant discrete graph generation models.
